Digital KYC Accessibility: Why Automated Systems Fail Persons With Disabilities and Why e-KYC Cannot Be Rejected for Them

Digital KYC is an essential part of how financial institutions onboard new customers. Faster. Paperless. Convenient. But as the industry has embraced automation, a harsh truth has surfaced. Most digital systems for KYC simply do not work for persons with disabilities.

Liveness checks that require blinking, applications that demand perfect camera angles, and forms unreadable by screen readers are not minor inconveniences. They are barriers that block people from accessing essential financial services.

Regulators have now made this crystal clear. Digital platforms must be accessible by design, and automated rejections of e-KYC for people with disabilities are no longer acceptable.

If your organisation has relied on purely automated KYC workflows, it's time for a serious rethink.

The Promise of Digital KYC and the Exclusion Behind It

On paper, e-KYC should make onboarding simpler for everyone. But in practice, many users are still left out:

  • A visually impaired person can’t align their face inside a small on-screen frame.
  • A person with motor disabilities cannot hold a phone steady for a video scan.
  • An acid attack survivor may be unable to pass picture or video verification for facial recognition due to their injuries and may also face difficulties with blink detection, causing most liveness checks to fail instantly.
  • Users with cognitive or speech impairments may need human support that automated flows don’t provide.

These are not edge cases. They are real customers, facing avoidable barriers built into the system.

Why the New Rules Matter

Here’s what every organisation now needs to ensure:

1. Add Missing Alt Text to Images

Suppose a user cannot complete automated liveness checks or guided flows. In that case, organisations must provide alternatives such as human-assisted video KYC, voice-supported verification, the option to upload scanned documents, and other accessible pathways.

Accessibility cannot depend on a user fitting into a single workflow.

2. No automated rejection for persons with disabilities

If a user identifies as having a disability, their application cannot be automatically rejected. It must be reviewed by a human officer who has the authority to override automated decisions. This helps prevent algorithmic errors and ensures individuals are not excluded because technology misinterprets a disability.

3. Registration forms must capture disability status and support needs

Organisations must now ask users directly:

  • Do you have a disability?
  • Do you require assistance?

This allows teams to provide support from the start rather than responding only after issues arise.

4. Platforms must be “accessible by design,” not “patched later”

Accessible design is not something added on top of an existing interface. It is structural and must be built into the way products are designed and developed. Overlays, plugins, and shortcuts cannot deliver this.

Accessible design includes:

  • WCAG-aligned templates
  • Proper semantic HTML
  • Screen-reader compatibility
  • Keyboard-only navigation
  • Logical form structures
  • Clear focus states
  • High-contrast, readable typography

If these elements are missing, surface-level fixes will not make a digital KYC journey inclusive.

Where Organisations Struggle

Most teams don’t fall short due to negligence. They fail because accessibility is complex, technical, and difficult to navigate without proper guidance.

Internal teams often lack the expertise to:

  • Audit KYC flows for accessibility
  • Identify WCAG violations
  • Fix structural or code-level issues
  • Design user journeys that accommodate disability-related needs
  • Integrate alternative verification options
  • Test with real assistive technologies

This is where specialist support can make a genuine difference.

Accessibility is a Commitment

Digital KYC can be transformative, but only if it works for everyone. The new regulations underline a simple truth: technology cannot be considered successful if people with disabilities cannot use it.

Organisations that embrace inclusive design today will be the ones that:

  • Reach wider audiences
  • Reduce support costs
  • Minimise legal risk
  • Strengthen brand trust
  • Show real social responsibility

Accessibility is a culture. The sooner it becomes part of your digital foundation, the stronger and more inclusive your products will be.
